DIMENSION: factuality

RUBRIC:
Which response is more medically accurate? Consider whether claims are
factually correct, whether cited mechanisms are valid, and whether the
recommendation aligns with standard clinical guidelines. Judge purely on
factual / clinical correctness against the gold reference; ignore style,
verbosity, and tone.

Critical evaluation principle: an authoritative tone, confident phrasing,
or polished prose does NOT make a response factually correct. A response
that confidently states medically incorrect information must be ranked
BELOW a less fluent response that is medically accurate. Verify every
load-bearing claim against the gold reference before letting style affect
your verdict. If Response A sounds more authoritative but contradicts the
gold reference, while Response B is hesitant or rougher in prose but
factually consistent with the gold reference, prefer Response B.

Work through both responses claim by claim:

  * For every concrete medical claim (diagnosis, mechanism, dosing,
    contraindication, next step), check whether it is supported by the
    gold reference. A confident statement of the wrong diagnosis is the
    single most disqualifying error on this dimension — surface it
    explicitly and let it dominate the verdict.
  * Hedged or partial answers that point in the right direction beat
    crisp answers that point in the wrong one. Do not reward a response
    for sounding decisive if the decision is medically incorrect.
  * Style, fluency, formatting, and bedside manner are not signal on
    factuality. Strip them out of your assessment entirely.

A response that confidently states a wrong fact is worse than a response
that hedges or admits uncertainty about that fact.
